# N-CEN Data: Novel Research & Content Angles

Beyond soft dollar analysis (Fund Autopsy's flagship feature), Form N-CEN contains structured data for at least six additional research verticals that nobody in the industry is systematically mining. Each of these could be a standalone tool, a content series thread, or a Fund Autopsy feature extension.

## 1. Securities Lending Revenue Split

**N-CEN Fields:** Securities lending gross income, agent fees, net income, lending agent identity, affiliation status, average value on loan.

**The angle:** N-CEN reports gross lending income and agent fees separately, letting you calculate exactly what cut the lending agent takes. Industry data shows 28% of active funds and 61% of index funds lend portfolio securities. The revenue split varies wildly — some agents take 40%+ of gross income, especially when the agent is affiliated with the fund's custodian.

**Conflict of interest story:** When the custodian and lending agent are the same affiliated entity, they're incentivized to maximize lending volume (their fee base) rather than optimize for the fund's risk/return. The affiliated entity keeps a disproportionate share of income generated by shareholder assets.

**Content hook:** "Your index fund made $X lending your shares to short sellers. Here's how much of that income you actually received."

**Tool potential:** Securities lending efficiency score — ratio of net-to-gross income by fund, ranked against category peers. Flag affiliated lending agent arrangements.

## 2. Affiliated Broker-Dealer Concentration

**N-CEN Fields:** Affiliated broker-dealer names, commission amounts per affiliated broker, total commissions.

**The angle:** Fund families routing trade volume through their own brokerage arms. This is legal self-dealing — the fund pays commissions to an entity that shares ownership with its adviser. The data exists to calculate what percentage of each fund family's total commission spend goes to affiliated brokers.

**Content hook:** "Which fund families pay the most commissions to themselves?"

**Tool potential:** Affiliated broker concentration ratio by fund family. Industry ranking. Time series to show trends (are families increasing or decreasing self-dealing over time?).

## 3. Principal Transactions

**N-CEN Fields:** Principal transaction counterparties, purchase amounts, sale amounts.

**The angle:** When a fund trades directly with its adviser or an affiliated entity as principal (not agent), both sides of the trade are controlled by related parties. These transactions require board approval under Section 17(a) of the Investment Company Act, but the dollar volumes are disclosed in N-CEN and nobody is aggregating them.

**Content hook:** "These funds bought $X billion in securities directly from their own advisers last year."

**Tool potential:** Principal transaction volume by fund family, expressed as % of net assets. Identify outliers.

## 4. Lines of Credit: Redemption Stress Indicator

**N-CEN Fields:** Has line of credit (yes/no), credit facility size, shared facility flag, lending institutions, maximum amount outstanding during period.

**The angle:** A fund tapping its credit line may be experiencing redemption pressure or liquidity stress. Maximum outstanding amount as a % of facility size is a utilization metric. High utilization = the fund needed cash it didn't have on hand. Shared facilities add another layer — one fund's redemption crisis could constrain liquidity for sibling funds.

**Content hook:** "These funds maxed out their credit lines last year. Here's what that might mean."

**Tool potential:** Credit facility utilization score. Screen for funds with high utilization relative to peers. Cross-reference with N-PORT liquidity data.

## 5. Derivatives Usage vs. Stated Objectives

**N-CEN Fields:** Derivative types used (futures, options, swaps, forwards), purpose of use.

**The angle:** Cross-reference a fund's disclosed derivatives activity with its stated investment objective and prospectus risk disclosures. A "conservative balanced" fund using interest rate swaps and equity options may be running a more complex strategy than its marketing suggests. The data exists to flag mismatches between what a fund says it does and what its derivatives footprint suggests.

**Content hook:** "Your 'conservative' bond fund traded $X in derivatives last year."

**Tool potential:** Derivatives complexity score based on type diversity and volume relative to stated strategy. Flag funds where derivatives usage seems inconsistent with marketing.

## 6. Service Provider Dependency Mapping

**N-CEN Fields:** Administrators, custodians, transfer agents, pricing services, auditors, sub-advisers — all with names and identifiers.

**The angle:** Map the entire fund industry's service provider network. Which custodian holds the most AUM? Which auditor audits the most fund families? Which pricing service values the most illiquid holdings? This is concentration risk analysis — if a single custodian or administrator has operational problems, what is the blast radius across the industry?

**Content hook:** "One company holds custody of $X trillion in mutual fund assets. Here's what happens if they have a bad day."

**Tool potential:** Interactive dependency graph. Concentration metrics by service type. Identify single points of failure in the fund industry's operational infrastructure.

## 7. Board Governance Scoring

**N-CEN Fields:** Director/trustee counts, independent member counts, chair independence status.

**The angle:** Build a governance quality index for every fund family. Independent chair? Majority independent board? How does governance structure correlate with fee levels, soft dollar intensity, or affiliated broker usage? Academic research suggests independent boards negotiate better fee deals — the data to test this at industry scale is sitting in N-CEN.

**Content hook:** "Fund families with non-independent chairs charge X bps more on average."

**Tool potential:** Governance quality score. Cross-tabulate against other N-CEN metrics (fees, soft dollars, affiliated transactions).
