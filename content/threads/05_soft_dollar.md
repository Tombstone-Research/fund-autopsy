# Thread: The Soft Dollar Subsidy

**Hook:** Your mutual fund pays inflated brokerage commissions so the fund manager can get "free" research. The manager keeps the full management fee. You pay twice.

---

**1/**
When a mutual fund trades, it pays brokerage commissions. That's expected. What's less expected is that a significant portion of those commissions aren't paying for trade execution at all.

They're paying for research. Bloomberg terminals. Analyst reports. Data feeds. Conference access.

**2/**
These are called soft dollar arrangements. The fund directs trades to brokers who bundle research services with execution. The broker charges a higher commission, and in return, provides research to the fund manager at no direct cost to the manager.

The cost is indirect. It comes out of the fund's assets. Your money.

**3/**
Here's where it gets interesting. The fund manager already charges a management fee, typically 0.50% to 1.00% of assets. That fee is supposed to cover the cost of managing the portfolio, which includes research.

But through soft dollars, the manager offloads a portion of their research costs onto the fund itself, through inflated commissions. They collect the full management fee AND get their research paid for by shareholders.

**4/**
The SEC requires disclosure of soft dollar arrangements in Form N-CEN. Items C.6.b and C.6.c report exactly how much of a fund's brokerage commissions went to soft dollar research services.

Academic estimates suggest soft dollars account for roughly 45% of total brokerage commissions across the industry. On a fund paying $5 million in annual commissions, that's $2.25 million in research costs passed to shareholders.

**5/**
The defense from the industry is that soft dollar research benefits the fund's performance. Better research leads to better stock picks.

The academic evidence is mixed at best. Erzurumlu and Kotomin (2016) found no consistent relationship between soft dollar usage and fund performance. The research benefit may exist, but it doesn't reliably show up in returns.
