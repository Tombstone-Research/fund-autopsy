# Thread: Revenue Sharing — Pay for Shelf Space

**Hook:** When your financial advisor recommends a mutual fund, it might be because it's the best option for you. Or it might be because the fund company paid your advisor's firm for the placement.

---

**1/**
The mutual fund industry has a distribution problem. There are thousands of funds competing for attention. Getting onto a brokerage platform's recommended list, a 401k menu, or an advisor's shortlist is everything.

Fund companies solve this with revenue sharing agreements — direct payments to the broker-dealers and platforms that sell their funds.

**2/**
This is not a conspiracy theory. It's disclosed. The fund's Statement of Additional Information describes these arrangements. The broker-dealer's Form ADV Part 2A does too.

But the disclosures are buried across two separate regulatory filings that almost nobody reads, written in language designed to be technically compliant without being practically useful.

**3/**
Here's what a typical revenue sharing arrangement looks like:

The fund company pays the broker-dealer a basis point fee on assets held on the platform. More assets, more payments. The broker-dealer, in turn, features that fund on their platform, recommended list, or model portfolio.

Your advisor may not even know the arrangement exists. It operates at the firm level.

**4/**
This matters because it distorts the recommendation process. An advisor working with a $500B platform might see 15 fund families on their preferred list. Some of those families are there because they're excellent. Some are there because they wrote a check.

The investor has no practical way to tell the difference.

**5/**
The SEC has studied this. The GAO has published reports on it. FINRA Rule 2341 governs non-cash compensation arrangements. The data is scattered across multiple regulatory bodies and filing types.

Nobody has stitched it together into a per-fund disclosure that says: "this fund has revenue sharing arrangements with these platforms."

**6/**
We're building that. Fund Autopsy is parsing the SAI and cross-referencing Form ADV data to surface distribution conflicts alongside the cost analysis we already provide.

The question isn't just "what does this fund cost?" It's "why was this fund recommended to you in the first place?"
